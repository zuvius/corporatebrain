## 1. The Brand Story

### 1.1 The Origin Story (The "Why")

Every great brand has a founding wound — a moment of frustration so universal it becomes the brand's entire reason to exist.

**Corporate Brain's founding wound:**

> A 200-person company. A senior engineer leaves after 4 years. Her last day, her manager asks her to write down everything she knows. She spends two days writing. She covers maybe 15% of what she actually knows. The rest — the decisions, the failed experiments, the architecture trade-offs, the "we tried that in 2022 and here's why it didn't work" — disappears.  
> Three months later, the new hire makes the same architectural mistake she avoided in 2022. It costs $180,000 and 6 weeks of engineering time to fix.  
> **This happens at every company, every week, everywhere.**

That's not a productivity problem. That's not a search problem. That's a **civilization problem** — the human race's inability to transmit hard-won wisdom across time and people.

Corporate Brain exists because **companies are not as smart as the people in them**. The individual knows. The organization forgets.

---

### 1.2 The Story Arc (3 Acts)

The brand narrative should always follow this arc — across landing pages, emails, sales decks, and conversations:

**Act 1: The World Before (The Pain)**
> Your company has been building knowledge for years. It's in Slack, Notion, email, PDFs, Zoom recordings, people's heads. But when someone needs that knowledge — right now, to make a decision, to answer a client, to not repeat a mistake — they can't find it. They ask around. They spend hours digging. They give up and guess. Or worse: they do the work again from scratch.

**Act 2: The Villain (The Real Enemy)**
> The enemy isn't your team. It's not poor discipline or bad tooling. The enemy is **fragmentation**. Knowledge was created in the right tool for the right moment — Slack for quick decisions, Drive for documents, Notion for wikis — but those tools were never designed to talk to each other. And AI tools like ChatGPT don't know your company exists. You paste them fragments. They answer generically. They hallucinate your own history back at you.

**Act 3: The Hero (Corporate Brain)**
> What if your company had a memory? Not a wiki you have to maintain. Not a search engine you have to query just right. A *brain* — one that has read everything your company has ever produced, remembers it all permanently, and answers your questions with the exact source cited.  
> That's Corporate Brain. Your company's second brain. Sovereign, permanent, and alive.

---

### 1.3 The Brand Metaphor System

Corporate Brain uses **three layered metaphors** that should appear consistently across all touchpoints:

| Metaphor | What It Means | Where to Use It |
|---|---|---|
| **The Brain** | A living, thinking entity — not a database | Hero copy, product naming, icon |
| **Memory** | Permanent retention vs. ephemeral tools | Problem statements, value props |
| **Fabric** | Connective tissue that links everything | Technical descriptions, architecture |

**The brain metaphor is what makes this brand emotionally resonant.** A search engine is cold and mechanical. A brain is warm, alive, and personal. When users think "my company's brain," they feel ownership. That ownership is the moat.

## 2. Brand Personality & Voice

### 2.1 The Four Personality Axes

Corporate Brain sits at a precise position on four axes:

```
HUMAN ←————————————————[◆]——→ TECHNICAL
        (Warm, relatable, story-driven — not cold API docs)

CONFIDENT ←—[◆]————————————→ HUMBLE
        (Knows what it's solving — doesn't hedge excessively)

DIRECT ←[◆]—————————————————→ VERBOSE
        (Every sentence earns its place — no filler)

SERIOUS ←————————[◆]—————————→ PLAYFUL
        (Not a game — but not a funeral either; occasional wit is earned)
```

### 2.2 Voice Principles

**Principle 1: Say the scary thing first.**
> ❌ "Corporate Brain helps teams access information more efficiently across integrated platforms."  
> ✅ "Your company is forgetting everything. Corporate Brain stops that."

**Principle 2: Name the enemy.**
> ❌ "Improve your knowledge management."  
> ✅ "Stop losing institutional knowledge every time someone quits."

**Principle 3: Be specific, not superlative.**
> ❌ "10x faster knowledge retrieval."  
> ✅ "Answer in 4 seconds what used to take 47 minutes."

**Principle 4: Write for the person at 11pm.**
> The user who will pay is the one who has just spent 45 minutes searching for a document that doesn't exist anymore. Write for them at that moment. Not for the calm, rational buyer in a board meeting.

**Principle 5: End sentences with weight.**
> ❌ "Corporate Brain can help with your knowledge management needs."  
> ✅ "Your company's knowledge doesn't have to die with every resignation."

---

### 2.3 Tone by Context

| Context | Tone | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Hero headline** | Bold, declarative, slightly provocative | "Your company is leaking knowledge. Here's the fix." |
| **Feature descriptions** | Confident, specific, outcome-focused | "Every answer cites its source. No more 'trust me.'" |
| **Onboarding empty states** | Warm, encouraging, human | "Nothing here yet — but your first answer is 10 minutes away." |
| **Error messages** | Calm, helpful, never blaming | "That didn't work. Let's try a different approach." |
| **Email subject lines** | Curiosity + urgency, never clickbait | "The question your team asked 47 times last month" |
| **Admin dashboard** | Precise, data-forward, no fluff | "23 queries saved 5.4 hours this week." |
| **Sales / pricing page** | Transparent, confident, anti-sales | "Here's what it costs. No hidden fees. No sales call required." |

### 2.4 Words We Use vs. Words We Avoid

| We Say | We Don't Say |
|---|---|
| Knowledge | Data (too cold) |
| Your company's brain | AI assistant (too generic) |
| Sovereign | Secure (too compliance-bro) |

| Ask | Query (too technical in user-facing copy) |
| Connect your tools | Integrate your systems |
| Instant answers | Fast responses |
| Your team | Users / end users |

## 3. Brand Positioning

### 3.1 The Positioning Statement (Internal Use)

> **For** knowledge workers at companies with 10–5,000 employees  
> **Who** waste 2.5 hours/day searching for information trapped across disconnected tools  
> **Corporate Brain is** a sovereign AI knowledge fabric  
> **That** transforms every document, Slack thread, and meeting into a permanent, searchable, citable company memory  
> **Unlike** Glean (cloud-only, $30/seat, enterprise sales only) or ChatGPT (no company context, hallucination-prone)  
> **Because** your knowledge should live in your infrastructure, answer in your voice, and never leave with your employees.

---

### 3.3 The Positioning Ladder

Brand positioning works in layers from rational to emotional:

```
RATIONAL BOTTOM LAYER (Features)
────────────────────────────────
"Hybrid RAG with citations across 4 integrations,
multi-LLM routing, flat pricing, self-deployable"

FUNCTIONAL MIDDLE LAYER (Benefits)
────────────────────────────────────
"Find any answer in seconds. Stop wasting hours digging.
Never reinvent work that's already been done."

EMOTIONAL TOP LAYER (Identity)
────────────────────────────────────
"Your company finally has a memory.
Knowledge doesn't die when people leave.
You own your intelligence."

ASPIRATIONAL SUMMIT (Transformation)
────────────────────────────────────
"You built something worth remembering.
Corporate Brain makes sure it's never forgotten."
```

The best brand storytelling **starts at the top** (transformation) and earns the right to explain the bottom (features) only after the emotional hook is set. Most SaaS brands do this backwards.

## 4. Persona Deep Dives (Emotional Layer)

Beyond the functional personas in the strategic analysis, here is the **emotional truth** of each — the feeling they carry, what makes them open their wallet, and what kills the sale.

---

### Persona 1: Alex — "The Burned CTO"

**The feeling they carry daily:**
> *Quiet dread.* Every week, Alex watches his engineers rediscover problems that were already solved. He knows the answer exists somewhere — a Slack thread, a Notion page, a PR comment from 2022. But finding it takes longer than just solving it again. He's stopped asking because the search always fails. The institutional knowledge his company spent years building is functionally inaccessible. And he's going to leave too, one day.

**The moment they're most ready to buy:**
> The third time in a month a new engineer asks "why did we choose X over Y?" and nobody can answer correctly.

**What they need to *feel* from the brand:**
> *Recognition without judgment.* Don't tell Alex he's disorganized. Tell him the system was broken before he arrived. Corporate Brain doesn't fix his failure — it fixes an industry-wide infrastructure gap.

**The sentence that sells to Alex:**
> "Every architecture decision your team has ever made, searchable in 4 seconds. No Notion archaeology required."

**What kills the sale:**
> Overly polished enterprise-sales energy. Alex has been burned by tools that promised everything in a demo and delivered complexity. He needs to see it work in 10 minutes, on his own, or he's gone.

**CTA that converts:** "Connect your GitHub + Slack → Ask your first question" (frictionless, no sales call)

---

### Persona 2: Priya — "The Compliance-Terrified CISO"

**The feeling they carry daily:**
> *Controlled panic.* Priya is being pressured from above (CEO bought Copilot licenses) and below (engineers using ChatGPT for code review). She knows data is leaving. She can't stop the tide — but she can direct it. She needs to say "yes to AI" without saying "yes to liability."

**The moment they're most ready to buy:**
> After a board question: "Do we have data sovereignty for our AI tools?" and she has to say "not fully."

**What they need to *feel* from the brand:**
> *Safety and control.* Not excitement. Not "wow, AI!" energy. Priya needs to feel that Corporate Brain is the *responsible* choice. The choice she can defend in an audit.

**The sentence that sells to Priya:**
> "Deploy entirely in your own infrastructure. Your documents never reach our servers. We can't see your data even if we wanted to."

**What kills the sale:**
> Any ambiguity about where data goes. Any marketing hype that feels like it's hiding complexity. Priya will read the ToS, the architecture diagram, and the data flow documentation. All three must be spotless.

**CTA that converts:** "Read our security architecture" (before asking for email/trial — she needs to trust before she tries)

---

### Persona 3: Marcus — "The Overwhelmed Ops Manager"

**The feeling they carry daily:**
> *Invisible exhaustion.* Marcus is the person everyone comes to because he knows where everything is. He's proud of being the one who knows — but he's also resentful of it. He answers the same 20 questions every week. He knows there's a document that answers each one. He spends more time pointing to documents than actually doing his job.

**The moment they're most ready to buy:**
> Friday afternoon, 4:45pm. His 9th Slack DM of the day: "Hey Marcus, where's the onboarding checklist?"

**What they need to *feel* from the brand:**
> *Liberation.* That Corporate Brain will take the "human search engine" burden off his shoulders. He'll still be the expert. But he won't have to be the directory anymore.

**The sentence that sells to Marcus:**
> "Answer every recurring question once. Let Corporate Brain answer it 10,000 times after that."

**What kills the sale:**
> Technical complexity. Marcus is not an engineer. If the onboarding asks him to configure pgvector or set up OAuth keys manually, he's done. Self-serve must be genuinely self-serve: upload a document → ask a question → get an answer.

**CTA that converts:** "Upload your team's top 5 documents. Ask a question. See it work." (Under 5 minutes.)

---

### Persona 4: Zara — "The Frustrated Associate"

**The feeling they carry daily:**
> *Imposter syndrome + inefficiency.* Zara is smart and ambitious but constantly feels like she's the only one who can't find things quickly. She doesn't realize everyone else is also spending hours searching. She thinks there's a trick she hasn't learned yet. The hours she wastes searching make her feel less capable than she is.

**The moment they're most ready to buy:**
> When she misses a client deadline because she was rebuilding a model that already existed in someone's SharePoint folder nobody told her about.

**What they need to *feel* from the brand:**
> *It's not you, it's the system.* Corporate Brain should make Zara feel smarter, not less — by giving her access to knowledge her colleagues have been hoarding by accident.

**The sentence that sells to Zara:**
> "Find the work that's already been done. Stop starting from zero."

**Note:** Zara is not the buyer — she's the champion. She'll send the link to her manager, post about it on LinkedIn, and become an internal advocate. Design for her word-of-mouth.

**CTA that converts:** "Try it on your last 3 projects. See what you missed." (Curiosity-driven, not commitment-driven)

---

### Persona 5: David — "The MSP Opportunity"

**The feeling they carry daily:**
> *Opportunity anxiety.* Every client is asking about AI. David doesn't have a good answer yet. He's watching competitors offer "AI consulting" and winning RFPs he's losing. He knows he needs to add AI to his service catalog — but he doesn't want to build it from scratch or resell something that doesn't fit his multi-client model.

**The moment they're most ready to buy:**
> When he loses a client RFP to a competitor who demonstrated an "AI knowledge assistant" in the demo.

**What they need to *feel* from the brand:**
> *Speed to market.* David wants to be able to say "we offer this" by next quarter. He needs a platform he can deploy for multiple clients, with data isolation between them, that he can put his brand on.

**The sentence that sells to David:**
> "Deploy white-labeled AI knowledge management for every client. Full data isolation. One platform."

**What kills the sale:**
> Single-tenant limitations. If the platform can't separate Client A's data from Client B's — it's a dealbreaker. Multi-tenancy must be the very first thing demonstrated.

**CTA that converts:** "Service Provider Program — Book a 30-min architecture call" (Different CTA than self-serve — he needs a conversation)

## 5. Messaging Architecture

### 5.1 The Messaging Hierarchy

Every piece of copy — every headline, CTA, email, tooltip — should trace back to this hierarchy:

```
LEVEL 1 — CATEGORY CLAIM (What we are)
"The AI Knowledge Fabric for the Modern Enterprise"

LEVEL 2 — DIFFERENTIATION CLAIM (Why us specifically)
"The only sovereign, multi-tenant platform with transparent pricing"

LEVEL 3 — PROOF POINTS (Why believe us)
• "Deploy in your own infrastructure — zero data egress"
• "Flat pricing — add 500 users without 500x the cost"
• "Cited answers — every response links to its source"
• "10-minute setup — no sales call, no implementation consultant"

LEVEL 4 — EMOTIONAL RESONANCE (Why care)
• "Your knowledge doesn't have to die when people leave"
• "Stop being the human search engine for your team"
• "Make every employee as smart as your best employee"
```

---

### 5.2 Hero Headline Variants (A/B Test Candidates)

The current hero headline:
> "All your work, all powered by AI"

This is **generic**. It could be any AI tool. Here are stronger alternatives ranked by emotional intensity:

| Rank | Headline | Why It Works |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **"Your company has been forgetting everything. Until now."** | Opens a wound, closes with hope |
| 2 | **"The answer exists. It's just buried in Slack from 2022."** | Hyper-specific pain → recognition |
| 3 | **"Every document your team has ever written. Every answer they've ever needed. One place."** | Scope + resolution |
| 4 | **"Stop losing institutional knowledge. Start building institutional memory."** | Loss aversion + aspirational |
| 5 | **"ChatGPT doesn't know your company. Corporate Brain does."** | Direct competitive displacement |
| 6 | **"Your company's second brain — sovereign, permanent, and always available."** | Emotional ownership language |

**Recommended A/B test:** Variant 1 vs. Variant 5 (wound-based vs. competitor-displacement)

---

### 5.3 Sub-headline & Body Copy Guidelines

**Sub-headline (below hero):**
> Should answer: "And here's the one thing you need to know about how."

Current sub-headline:
> "Corporate Brain connects your docs, conversations, and knowledge into one intelligent system."

Stronger alternative:
> "Connect Slack, Drive, Notion, and your documents. Ask anything. Get cited answers. Setup takes 10 minutes — no sales call required."

**Why:** Specificity (real tools), social proof (10 minutes), anti-friction (no sales call).

Every page should have a primary CTA and a secondary CTA serving different psychological states:

| Persona State | Primary CTA | Secondary CTA |

| Ready to try | "Start Free — No Card Required" | "See a 2-min Demo" |
| Skeptical | "See How It Works" | "Read Customer Stories" |

| Enterprise buyer | "Request Enterprise Pricing" | "Download ROI Calculator" |

### 5.5 Microcopy Standards

These small words have outsized conversion impact:

| Element | Current | Better | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Primary CTA | "Get Started" | "Start Free — No Card Required" | Removes friction anxiety |
| Form field | "Email" | "Work Email" | Sets expectation, improves lead quality |
| Form submit | "Sign Up" | "Create Your Workspace" | Action-oriented, creates mental ownership |

| Error state | "Something went wrong" | "That didn't work — let's try again" | Human, not robotic |
| Pricing page | "Custom" | "Let's talk — we'll find the right fit" | Collaborative, not bureaucratic |

## 6. Storytelling Frameworks

### 6.1 The Before/After/Bridge Framework

Use this for every feature description, every email, every case study:

**Before:** State the painful current reality (specific, not abstract)  
**After:** Paint the ideal future state (emotional, not feature-listed)  
**Bridge:** Corporate Brain is how you get from Before to After

**Example — for the Knowledge Sidebar feature:**

> **Before:** You upload a PDF. You don't know if it processed. You ask a question. You get an answer. You don't know if that answer came from your PDF or the AI's general training. You can't verify it.
>
> **After:** You upload a PDF. A status indicator shows "Indexed — 47 chunks." You ask a question. The answer appears with a citation: "Source: Q3_Strategy.pdf, page 4." You click the citation. You read the exact paragraph. You trust the answer.
>
> **Bridge:** Corporate Brain's citation engine traces every answer to its source. Not a file name — the specific excerpt. Trust isn't assumed. It's earned, one citation at a time.

---

### 6.2 The "Day in the Life" Narrative

The most powerful B2B storytelling is a day-in-the-life that prospects recognize themselves in. Use across website, demo scripts, and sales emails:

---

**8:47 AM.** Arjun opens Slack. Three DMs already. One from a junior engineer: "Hey, do you know where the database schema decisions doc is? I'm about to make a change and want to make sure I'm not repeating something we already tried."

Arjun doesn't know. He types back: "Check Notion, maybe the Architecture folder?" He knows it's probably not there.

**9:12 AM.** The junior engineer replies: "Can't find it. I'll just look at the code." Arjun feels a familiar weight. This is going to be a problem. He just doesn't know when.

---

*Three months later:*

**8:47 AM.** Arjun opens Slack. Three DMs already. One from a junior engineer: "Hey, I checked Corporate Brain — looks like we tried this approach in March 2023 and hit this exact issue. Linking the thread. I'll take a different approach."

Arjun stares at the screen for a second. He didn't know Corporate Brain had indexed that Slack thread. He didn't know the junior engineer even knew how to use it. He takes a sip of coffee.

*This is what institutional memory feels like.*

---

### 6.3 The "Villain Framing" — What to Do With Fragmentation

Great brand storytelling needs an external villain — something to fight against that isn't the customer's fault.

**Corporate Brain's villain: Fragmentation**

Not the customer. Not the team. Not even the tool vendors. The villain is the *system architecture* of modern work — where the best tool for each moment creates an archipelago of isolated knowledge islands.

> "Your team didn't fail to document. Your Slack is full of decisions. Your Drive is full of documents. Your Notion is full of wikis. The problem isn't that the knowledge doesn't exist. The problem is that it exists in 14 places that don't talk to each other. You don't have a documentation problem. You have a fragmentation problem."

This framing is generous to the customer (they're not to blame) and positions Corporate Brain as the hero that fights the structural enemy.

## 7. Micro-interactions Playbook

Micro-interactions are the moments where the brand *becomes a feeling* rather than a concept. Every micro-interaction is a brand touchpoint.

### 7.1 Philosophy: Every Animation Has a Job

Corporate Brain's micro-interactions should follow one rule:

> **Every animation earns its existence by reducing confusion, confirming completion, or building delight — never for vanity.**

| Type | Purpose | Rule |
|---|---|---|
| **Feedback animations** | Confirm an action worked | Immediate (< 100ms response start) |
| **Progress animations** | Show background work happening | Meaningful (show what's actually happening, not a generic spinner) |
| **Delight animations** | Reward a milestone | Earned (only on genuine completion moments) |
| **Transition animations** | Orient during state changes | Spatial (slide in the direction of logical navigation) |

---

### 7.2 The Chat Interface

**Message Send:**
- User types → sends → message slides in from right-bottom with a subtle `ease-out` curve (not a pop, not a fade — a glide)
- The input clears with a micro-fade (30ms) and a gentle haptic pulse (mobile)
- AI response area shows a three-dot `...` typing indicator with a violet pulse — not generic gray dots
- The indicator disappears and the response streams in token-by-token (once streaming is built — see FEATURES_AUDIT.md A1)
- **Current state:** Full response dumps at once. The "thinking" gap is empty. This feels slow and broken.

**Citation Hover:**
- Hovering a citation in the sidebar should show a tooltip with the exact excerpt — a 200px wide card sliding in from the right with the source title, page number, and highlighted excerpt
- The card has a `border-l-4 border-violet-400` treatment and faint `bg-violet-500/5` background
- **Current state:** Citations show only document titles. No preview, no excerpt.

**Model Indicator:**
- Below each AI response: a pill showing `gemini-1.5-flash · 0.6s · $0.0001` in `text-white/30`
- On hover: expands to show why this model was selected ("Fast mode — query under 50 words")
- **Current state:** Not implemented.

---

### 7.3 Document Upload Flow

**Drag & Drop:**
- On file hover over drop zone: border animates to `border-violet-400` with a violet glow (`box-shadow: 0 0 0 3px rgba(139,92,246,0.3)`)
- Drop zone background shifts from `bg-white/[0.02]` to `bg-violet-500/10`
- The upload icon does a single rotation (180deg, `ease-out`, 300ms) to confirm "received"
- **Current state:** Static border change only.

**Processing Progress:**
- Instead of a generic spinner: show a 4-step pipeline progress bar  
  `Uploading → Parsing → Chunking → Indexing`
- Each step shows a checkmark when complete, with a subtle green flash (`text-emerald-400`)
- The step label animates in: "Parsing 47 pages..." → "Chunking into 312 segments..." → "Indexing with embeddings..."
- **Current state:** Status field in DB exists (`pending` → `indexed`) but UI shows nothing meaningful.

**Duplicate Detection:**
- If SHA-256 hash matches an existing document: the upload card shakes once (150ms, 5px right-left) and shows a warning badge: "Already indexed · Uploaded 3 days ago"
- Not a blocking error — an inline warning with "Upload anyway" option
- **Current state:** Hash check is built on backend. Frontend shows a toast but not inline card feedback.

---

### 7.4 Integration Connection Flow

**Connect Button:**
- Default: `border border-white/20 bg-white/5 text-white/70`
- Hover: `border-violet-400/50 bg-violet-500/10 text-white` — the icon animates a 1-pulse scale (1.0 → 1.1 → 1.0, 200ms)
- Click: button transforms to a loading state showing the provider logo animating in a circle (like OAuth pending)
- OAuth window opens → user authorizes → window closes → button state transitions to `Connected ✓` with a green checkmark that draws in (SVG path animation, 400ms)
- **Current state:** Static state changes only. No animation on connect.

**Sync Indicator:**
- When a sync is running: a subtle progress ring appears around the integration icon
- The ring fills over the estimated sync duration
- On completion: a brief "Synced — 847 new items" toast, then the ring pulses once and disappears
- **Current state:** No visual sync progress.

---

### 7.5 Onboarding Flow

**Step transitions:**
- Each step slides in from the right (`translateX(40px) → translateX(0)`, `ease-out`, 350ms)
- Previous step slides out to the left (`translateX(0) → translateX(-40px)`)
- Progress indicator dots (current = violet, complete = white, upcoming = white/20)
- **Current state:** Steps appear/disappear without transition.

**Completion celebration:**
- On Step 4 (Complete): a single burst of violet/fuchsia particles (confetti) from the center of the screen — 40 particles, 600ms duration, `ease-out` gravity
- The "Start using Corporate Brain" button pulses once (scale 1.0 → 1.04 → 1.0) after 800ms delay
- **Current state:** Static completion screen, no celebration.

---

### 7.6 The ⌘K Omnibox

**Open state:**
- The omnibox opens with a subtle backdrop blur (`backdrop-blur-xl`) that fades in at 150ms
- The input field focuses with a violet underline that draws in from left-to-right (300ms)
- Recent queries animate in staggered (each 40ms delayed from previous)

**Search typing:**
- As the user types, results filter in real-time with a fast fade (80ms)
- Matching text in results is highlighted in `text-violet-300`
- The selected item has a `bg-violet-500/15` background that slides up/down following keyboard navigation (layout animation)
- **Current state:** 4 static suggestions hardcoded. No dynamic results, no recent queries.

**Execute:**
- On Enter: the omnibox closes with a reverse blur-out (150ms)
- The chat input populates with the selected text via a typewriter effect (10ms per character)
- **Current state:** Closes immediately, no transition.

---

### 7.7 Data Visualization (Context Map)

The Context Map is currently the biggest brand deception — it looks impressive but shows random fake data. Once connected to the real knowledge graph API:

**Node hover:**
- Hovering a node: it scales to 1.3x with a violet glow ring appearing (300ms, ease-out)
- Connected nodes highlight (opacity 1.0 vs. 0.3 for unconnected)
- A tooltip appears above: Document title, type icon, citation count, last queried date

**Node click:**
- Clicking a node opens a right sidebar panel with the document preview and recent citations
- The node "centers" in the visualization with neighboring nodes redistributing (D3 force update, 500ms)

**Connection lines:**
- Animate from opacity 0 to opacity (strength × 0.8) on page load, staggered by 50ms
- Stronger connections (more co-citations) render thicker and brighter

---

### 7.8 Error States

Errors are a brand moment. Done wrong, they break trust. Done right, they build it.

**Rule:** Every error state must have:
1. What happened (specific, not generic)
2. Why (if knowable)
3. What to do next (exact action)

| Error | ❌ Current | ✅ Better |
|---|---|---|
| Upload fail | "Upload failed" | "This PDF couldn't be parsed — it may be scanned or image-only. Try uploading a text-searchable version or enable Unstructured.io for OCR support." |
| Integration disconnect | "Failed to connect" | "Slack authorization failed — your token may have expired. Click to reconnect and re-authorize." |
| No results | "No results found" | "No matching knowledge yet. Try uploading documents or ask a broader question." |
| API timeout | "Something went wrong" | "The AI took too long to respond. This usually fixes itself — try again in a moment." |

## Summary: The Brand Stack

```
STORY         → "Companies forget. Corporate Brain remembers."
POSITIONING   → Sovereign + Transparent + Multi-tenant (the unoccupied corner)
VOICE         → Direct. Specific. Human. Never a feature list.
PERSONAS      → Burned CTO / Terrified CISO / Overwhelmed Ops / Frustrated Associate / MSP Builder
MICRO-UX      → Every interaction confirms, every transition orients, every error teaches
VISUAL BRAND  → Deep dark (#0a0a0f) + violet-to-fuchsia gradient + Nunito + Lucide
THE GAP       → Marketing beauty hasn't reached the app yet. Fix that first.
```

**The single most important brand action right now:**  
The marketing site tells a beautiful story. The moment a user signs up and enters the app, that story collapses into a gray, static, unanimated interface. **The brand promise and the brand experience are misaligned.** Closing that gap — dark theme in app, streaming responses, animated upload progress, real context map — is the highest-leverage brand investment.
